This is already the "hedged FEP correspondence" I would have recommended adding. v6.8 did it.

## Real gaps — what v6.9 should actually do

### Gap 1: Markov-blanket framing for fleet structure (§2 / §6)

v6.8 treats heterogeneous agent fleets as a class-structured population (resident vs. ephemeral, different compute/memory profiles, class-conditional calibration). The FEP-native way to frame this is as **nested Markov blankets**: each agent class is a distinct blanket with partial autonomy; resident agents are long-lived blankets with rich internal state; ephemeral agents are short-lived blankets that exchange with the environment and dissolve. The fleet-as-system has an outer blanket (the governance substrate) enclosing inner blankets (individual agents).

This is a pure exposition move — no new math required. It connects the heterogeneity argument (which is currently presented as an engineering/safety claim) to an active-inference-compatible structural story. It also tightens the ephemeral/resident distinction by giving it theoretical weight instead of just operational weight.

**What to write:** One new subsection in §2 ("Heterogeneous Agent Fleets"), ~400 words, introducing Markov blankets and mapping the class structure onto nested-blanket topology. Cite Kirchhoff et al. 2018 ("The Markov blankets of life") or Friston 2013 for the formalism.

**Risk:** Markov-blanket formalism attracts controversy in FEP-critical circles (Biehl, Raja; recent arguments that Markov blankets are over-claimed for biological systems). Hedge: "we use Markov-blanket vocabulary as structural scaffolding, not as an ontological claim that each agent is a minimum-free-energy Markov blanket in Friston's strict sense."

### Gap 2: Active inference named as the missing derivation (§4 or §15)

v6.8 defers full variational −F to future work but does not explicitly name **active inference** as the framework that would close the loop between monitoring (passive observation of F) and governance (interventions that minimize expected free energy). The governance verdicts (`guide`, `pause`, `reject`) are currently presented as threshold-triggered control actions; under active inference, they would be recast as policy selections minimizing G (expected free energy over future trajectories).

**What to write:** A short paragraph in §15 "Conclusion" or a dedicated §X.Y "Toward Active-Inference Governance" subsection, ~300 words. State explicitly:
- Current verdicts = threshold-triggered, not derived from EFE minimization
- A principled extension would replace the CIRS v2 control law with an active-inference policy
- This requires per-agent generative models, which is v7+ scope
- Do NOT claim the current verdicts are active-inference actions — they aren't.

**Risk:** Overclaiming. If the drafting session writes anything that could be read as "verdicts are active-inference policies," a Friston-literate reviewer will note the absent G derivation. Honest framing is cheaper than cleanup.

### Gap 3: Calibration ↔ precision link (§9 → §4 cross-reference)

§9 "Class-Conditional Calibration" presents calibration as a Phase 2 measurement of scale constants. §4 frames E as "precision / negative free energy." The two sections never connect. Under FEP, **calibration deviation IS precision deviation** — an agent whose stated confidence doesn't match outcomes has mis-weighted prediction errors, and precision is exactly the weight on prediction error in variational dynamics.

**What to write:** A short remark or cross-reference in §9 (1–2 paragraphs), explicitly tying the class-conditional calibration protocol to precision weighting. This is a vocabulary tightening, not new math — the operational protocol stands; the FEP interpretation adds theoretical coherence.

**Payoff:** Makes the calibration story connect to a literature (active-inference precision-control) that currently doesn't know the paper exists.

### Gap 4: Resource-rate proxy — when does it correlate with −F?

v6.8 honestly flags the `E_resource` heuristic as "not equivalent to −F" but says nothing about *when* the proxy might be expected to correlate with −F and when it will diverge. A short remark would add rigor without commitment. Candidate claim: under stationary regime with a fixed generative model, resource throughput lower-bounds −F up to a scale constant (higher throughput = more evidence integration per unit time); under non-stationary regimes (context switches, model updates), the proxy is uninformative.

**Risk:** Requires actually thinking about the correlation claim rather than waving. If the drafting session can't back the stationarity claim with a derivation or citation, drop this gap entirely — it's the lowest-priority of the four.

## What v6.9 should NOT do

- **Do not claim EISV dynamics are derived from minimizing a specific variational free energy functional.** They aren't, and §4's honesty about this is a feature, not a bug.
- **Do not reframe the governance verdicts as active-inference policies.** They aren't; Gap 2 names this as future work.
- **Do not add a second FEP correspondence table.** §4.1 has it.
- **Do not remove the legacy-heuristic tier-3 fallbacks.** They are what makes the framework deployable at all; removing them would be a different paper.

## Positioning note for the drafter

v6's restraint — "we can verify the measurements; we hedge on deeper structure" — is a reviewer-facing asset. v6.9 FEP strengthening should preserve that character, not abandon it. The bar for every FEP claim in v6.9 is: can this be verified or is it post-hoc dressing? If verified, commit. If dressing, hedge explicitly. If neither, drop.
